This report separates what is **verified** from what is **directional**:

- **Verified (high confidence):** Claims fact-checked verbatim against primary sources (WEF/Accenture, WEF/BCG, McKinsey, Deloitte, Siemens, Kearney, Gartner). **The single most important caveat:** Most headline performance numbers (e.g., +40% productivity, –48% lead time, the NIO/Foxconn/Amazon results) come from the **WEF–McKinsey Global Lighthouse Network** and **WEF/BCG "Physical AI" showcases** — a self-selected, expert-adjudicated **elite of 238 sites out of millions of factories**. Read them as the *leading edge*, not the industry average. Broad-market evidence consistently shows the typical manufacturer is still stuck in "pilot purgatory." The leader-vs-laggard gap is the defining feature of the next decade.

## Executive summary

Over 2026–2036, manufacturing shifts from an **efficiency-led** operating philosophy to one of **adaptive resilience** — driven by compounding structural shocks (geopolitics, climate volatility, workforce shortages, cyber risk) and enabled by AI, simulation, robotics and hyperconnected infrastructure (WEF/Accenture, *Intelligent Industrial Operations Outlook 2026*).

Ten things to take away:

1. **The factory becomes "orchestrated," not just automated.** Accenture's 2040 vision is AI governing production in real time, digital twins modeling every decision before execution, and humanoid robots adapting without human intervention. McKinsey frames this as "software-defined automation" arriving in roughly **5–15 years**, depending on adoption speed.
2. **Transformation is real but concentrated.** The Global Lighthouse Network reached **238 advanced sites by June 2026** (35+ countries, 30+ industries). Leaders average **+40% labor productivity and –48% lead time**; most manufacturers still lag.
3. **Investment intent is strong, ambition is uneven.** Deloitte: **80%** of executives plan to put **≥20%** of improvement budgets into smart manufacturing. Accenture: **63%** prioritize automation mid-term, but only **38%** target a fully hyper-automated factory.
4. **Supply chains regionalize on paper faster than in practice.** Kearney's 2025 Reshoring Index fell a record **311 basis points**; US output grew just **~1%**. CEO reshoring *intent* rose **15% YoY** with a **~50%** jump in those citing geopolitics — but capacity lags announcements. The "China+1" reroute is the dominant real move (China imports fell ~⅓; other Asian low-cost countries absorbed the volume).
5. **The supply chain's nervous system goes agentic.** Gartner: SCM software with agentic-AI capability grows from **<$2B (2025) to $53B (2030)**, and **60%** of enterprises will adopt agentic features by 2030 (up from **5%**).
6. **Business models tilt from selling products to selling outcomes** — servitization, product-as-a-service and outcome-based pricing — economically viable now that IoT makes usage and outcomes measurable.
7. **Physical AI scales.** Manufacturer adoption of physical AI (autonomous mobile robots, humanoids) is forecast to **more than double in two years, 9% → 22%**. The model is a *layered* robotics strategy: rule-based, training-based and context-based systems coexisting.
8. **Frontier tech matures on different clocks.** Additive manufacturing (~$20.5B 2023 → ~$83B 2030), quantum computing (~$0.85B 2022 → ~$6.7B 2030), and in-space manufacturing (~$1.3B 2024 → ~$23.4B 2035) are all high-growth but at very different absolute scales and maturities.
9. **Work stays human-centered.** More than **81%** of manufacturing task-hours are expected to remain human-driven; **70%** of factory managers call workforce transformation *the* most critical success factor for 2040. WEF: **39%** of core skills change by 2030, and **zero** of 2,800+ skills assessed are at "very high" risk of GenAI substitution.
10. **Products and costing are reinvented at the design stage.** Generative/agentic AI compresses R&D from **years to months** (up to **–70%** prototype cycles in pharma; up to **–20% R&D cost** in medtech), enabling mass personalization — which in turn pushes costing from unit-cost thinking toward **cost-to-serve** and **value/outcome-based pricing**.

## 1. The new operating model: from efficiency to adaptive resilience

**What changes.** For a century the factory was optimized for **efficiency** — lowest unit cost at scale. The next decade reorients the core objective toward **adaptive resilience**: the ability to sense, simulate and reconfigure in real time against constant disruption. WEF and Accenture (*Intelligent Industrial Operations Outlook 2026*, April 2026) frame the transition along two dimensions:

**An autonomy axis** — four stages industrial systems progress through:

| Stage | What the system does | Human role |
|---|---|---|
| **Assisted** | AI recommends; humans decide and act | Operator-in-control |
| **Co-adaptive** | Humans and machines adjust to each other continuously | Collaborator |
| **Self-directing** | System sets and pursues sub-goals within guardrails | Supervisor / exception handler |
| **Self-managing** | System governs, heals and optimizes itself | Governor / strategist |

**A three-horizon timeline:**

- **NOW (0–2 yrs):** Early adoption of frontier tech; AI copilots, analytics, pilots at scale in leading sites.
- **NEAR (3–5 yrs):** Expanding human–machine collaboration; agents orchestrate multi-step workflows; digital twins move from monitoring to decisioning.
- **NEXT (5+ yrs):** A symbiotic, "living," near-autonomous ecosystem — demand-responsive rather than forecast-driven.

**Why now.** The driver is not technology pull alone but **structural shock**: geopolitics and tariffs, climate volatility, chronic workforce shortages, and cyber risk are forcing a redesign of how plants and networks operate (McKinsey, January 2026). AI, automation, simulation, robotics and hyperconnected infrastructure are simply what make a resilient response affordable.

**Automation maturity & "lights-out."** McKinsey's scenario analysis projects that today's most advanced capabilities — digital twins, self-teaching robots, self-programming devices — become **commonplace**, enabling **software-defined automation** in ~5–15 years. Among automation users surveyed, **94%** say digital solutions will be important to future efforts (up from 69%), with AI a strong enabler of nearly all key automation technologies. Fully "lights-out" (uncrewed) plants remain the exception and tend to be viable only for narrow, highly standardized, high-volume processes; the realistic 2036 norm is **"lights-dim"** — minimally crewed lines with humans concentrated in supervision, exception handling, maintenance and continuous improvement.

**Distributed / micro-manufacturing.** Additive manufacturing, modular cells and cloud-orchestrated production make **distributed, smaller-footprint, closer-to-demand** manufacturing economically credible for a growing set of parts — complementing (not replacing) mega-scale plants for commodity goods.

> **Bottom line:** The 2036 factory is less a fixed asset optimized once and more a **continuously re-optimizing system**. The competitive question shifts from "how cheaply can you make it?" to "how fast can you reconfigure when the world changes?"

## 2. Supply chains: global *and* local, digital and agentic

### 2.1 The reshoring reality check

Political momentum for reshoring is real, but the data says the physical economy moves slower than the rhetoric:

- Kearney's **2025 Reshoring Index fell by a record 311 basis points**, back into negative territory after two strong years. US manufacturing output grew just **~1%** — about half the rate of US demand growth — reflecting a **long lag between investment announcements and operational capacity** (Kearney, April 2025).
- Imports from 14 Asian low-cost countries/regions (LCCRs) rose **~$90B (10%)**, led by computers, electronics and electrical equipment. CEOs planning to reshore part of operations within three years rose **15% YoY**, and those citing **geopolitics** as a primary motivator jumped **~50%**.
- The follow-on **2026 Reshoring Index improved slightly (−115 → −86) but stayed negative.** The real structural move is a **reroute, not a return**: direct imports from mainland China fell **by almost one-third (–$135B)** while **other Asian LCCRs gained +$194B** — classic "China+1" diversification rather than wholesale reshoring. The manufacturing import ratio rose to **14.15%** (2025) vs 13.29% (2024).

**Implication for 2026–2036:** Expect a **multi-polar, regionalized** footprint — "in-region, for-region" hubs (North America, Europe, an Asia network increasingly centered on India/Vietnam/Mexico-as-gateway) layered over persistent global sourcing for the hardest-to-localize categories (advanced electronics, apparel). Localization will be **selective and category-specific**, gated by ecosystem readiness, energy, water and skilled labor — Kearney's blunt verdict: *"the next phase will require not just capital, but coordination."*

### 2.2 The digital, agentic supply network

The bigger near-term transformation is the supply chain's **operating intelligence**:

- Gartner forecasts **SCM software with agentic-AI capabilities grows from <$2B (2025) to $53B (2030)**, and that **60% of enterprises** using SCM software will have adopted agentic features by 2030 — **up from 5% in 2025** (Gartner, April 2026).
- The progression is **AI assistants → simple task agents → orchestrated clusters of agents** running multi-step workflows "with or without humans in the loop." Procurement criteria are shifting: AI-assistant features are becoming a *mandatory* selection requirement.
- Gartner's caution mirrors the factory story: **deployment will lag availability.** The gating constraints are **data management, operations management, workforce AI-readiness and network-centricity** — not the software itself.

**Resilience by design.** Digital supply networks (real-time, multi-tier visibility + digital-twin simulation of disruptions) replace linear, forecast-driven chains. Tech and regionalization are genuinely strengthening resilience — but McKinsey warns of **complacency**: as acute shocks fade from memory, investment in resilience risks stalling before it is structural.

> **Bottom line:** Globally, expect **regional resilience hubs + China+1 diversification**, not a great reshoring. Locally and digitally, expect an **agentic control tower** to become standard infrastructure — the supply chain's nervous system.

## 3. Business-model evolution: from selling products to selling outcomes

The decade's commercial shift is from **transactional product sales** toward **recurring, service- and outcome-based revenue**. Four overlapping models:

1. **Servitization / Product-as-a-Service (PaaS).** The manufacturer retains ownership and sells *access* or *availability* (e.g., "power by the hour," compressed-air-as-a-service, machines billed by uptime or units produced). IoT instrumentation is the enabler: usage and condition are now measurable in real time, so the product can be priced across its entire lifecycle rather than once at sale.
2. **Outcome-based monetization.** Deloitte's definition: the customer **"pays for a predefined tangible business outcome or value realized from services consumed."** The hard part is **defining and pricing the outcome**, which depends on understanding the **cost-to-serve** each customer — making granular usage data and AI-driven cost modeling prerequisites, not nice-to-haves.
3. **Manufacturing-as-a-Service (MaaS) & platform models.** Cloud-orchestrated, capacity-on-demand networks let buyers source production the way they source compute. Platform owners monetize orchestration, quality assurance and matching — shifting value from *owning* assets to *coordinating* them.
4. **Mass personalization.** Digital twins + flexible automation make **lot-size-one** economically viable. The exemplar: **NIO's Hefei plant** ran a digital-twin platform managing **3.6M+ vehicle configurations**, accelerating speed-to-market **44%** and automating **90% of R&D workflows** (WEF Lighthouse data). Personalization becomes a *default capability*, not a premium exception.

**Why models change:** when products become **software-defined and sensor-rich**, the margin migrates from the one-time hardware sale to the **ongoing relationship** — data, updates, optimization, outcomes. Incumbents that stay purely transactional cede the high-margin, defensible recurring layer to whoever owns the data and the platform.

> **Bottom line:** The winning 2036 manufacturer sells **capability and outcomes over a lifecycle**, underwritten by data it already collects. Pricing, finance and sales must be rebuilt around recurring, outcome-linked revenue.

## 4. The technology stack of the AI era

### 4.1 The intelligence layer: agentic AI, industrial foundation models, copilots

Siemens identifies **agentic AI** and **foundation models** as the defining industrial-AI trends through 2030:

- **Industrial Foundation Models (IFMs):** pre-trained on **industry-specific** data (sensor, process, engineering) to enable faster, more accurate deployment than general-purpose models.
- **Industrial Copilots:** generative-AI assistants that address skilled-labor shortages and accelerate human–machine collaboration. These are already in commercial deployment — Siemens' Industrial Copilot reached general availability in 2024 with 100+ companies (e.g., Schaeffler, thyssenkrupp) — with LLMs increasingly acting as "translators" between machines, APIs and people.
- **Agentic AI:** systems that don't just advise but **act** — executing discrete tasks and, increasingly, orchestrating multi-step workflows. In leading manufacturers, AI is moving **from isolated pilots to the governing intelligence layer**; **62%** of surveyed factory managers see AI as a key enabler across all operations (Accenture).

### 4.2 Physical AI and the layered robotics strategy

- Manufacturer adoption of **physical AI** (autonomous robots — mobile platforms, robotic "dogs," humanoids able to traverse unstructured floors) is forecast to **more than double, 9% → 22%, in two years** (Deloitte, citing the Manufacturing Leadership Council). *(Note: stated deployment intent, not measured deployment.)*
- The emerging operating model is a **layered automation strategy** of three coexisting robotics systems (WEF/BCG, *Physical AI*, Sept 2025):
  - **Rule-based** — structured, repetitive tasks (today's industrial robots).
  - **Training-based** — variable tasks learned via reinforcement/imitation learning.
  - **Context-based** — zero-shot execution in unpredictable or novel environments (the frontier; where humanoids and embodied foundation models are headed).
- **Exemplars:** **Foxconn** combined AI robotics + digital-twin simulation to automate high-precision tasks (screw tightening, cable insertion), cutting deployment time **40%** and operational cost **15%**. **Amazon**, running the world's largest robotics fleet, reports **25% faster delivery, 30% more skilled roles, and a 25% efficiency boost.**

### 4.3 Digital twins & simulation

Digital twins move from **monitoring** to **decisioning**: every significant change is modeled and stress-tested virtually before it touches the physical line. This is the connective tissue across the whole stack — it underwrites mass personalization (NIO), de-risks capital decisions, and lets agents "rehearse" actions safely.

### 4.4 Additive manufacturing (3D printing)

- Market: **~$20.5B (2023) → ~$83.1B (2030), CAGR ~20.6%** (volume CAGR ~22.9%) (NextMSC). Maturity: **scaling production tech**, moving from prototyping toward end-use parts, spares-on-demand and distributed manufacturing. Strategically it underpins **localization, lightweighting and design freedom**.

### 4.5 Quantum computing

- Market: **~$0.85B (2022) → ~$6.7B (2030), CAGR ~29.9%** (NextMSC). Maturity: **early/emerging.** The credible 2026–2036 manufacturing impact is concentrated in **materials discovery, molecular simulation, and complex optimization** (scheduling, routing, supply-chain and portfolio optimization). Expect **narrow, high-value pilots** via quantum-as-a-service through this decade rather than broad production use; treat quantum as a **watch-and-experiment** bet, not a near-term operational dependency.

### 4.6 Space-based / in-orbit manufacturing

- Market: **~$1.3B (2024) → ~$23.4B (2035), CAGR ~29.8%** (Market Research Future). Techniques include **3D printing, microgravity casting, chemical vapor deposition and molecular beam epitaxy**; early applications in specialized fiber optics, pharmaceuticals/bio, semiconductors and advanced materials that benefit from microgravity. Maturity: **nascent and niche**, gated by launch cost. Most relevant to **high-value, low-mass** products, not mainstream goods — but a genuine new frontier as launch economics improve (SpaceX, Blue Origin, et al.).

### 4.7 Advanced materials, IoT & edge

- **IoT/edge** is the **substrate** for everything above — it generates the proprietary data that trains IFMs, feeds digital twins and powers servitization. **Advanced materials** (often AI- and increasingly quantum-assisted in discovery) reshape what products *can be*, feeding directly into the design and costing changes in §10.

| Technology | Indicative market trajectory | Maturity (2026) | Primary 2026–2036 manufacturing impact |
|---|---|---|---|
| Agentic AI / IFMs / copilots | SCM agentic AI <$2B→$53B by 2030 | Scaling | Governing intelligence layer; workflow orchestration |
| Physical AI / robotics | Adoption 9%→22% (2 yrs) | Early-scaling | Flexible automation of unstructured tasks |
| Digital twins | Core enabler across stack | Scaling | Decisioning, personalization, capital de-risking |
| Additive manufacturing | ~$20.5B→~$83B by 2030 | Scaling | Distributed production, design freedom, spares |
| Quantum computing | ~$0.85B→~$6.7B by 2030 | Emerging | Materials, simulation, optimization (pilots) |
| In-space manufacturing | ~$1.3B→~$23.4B by 2035 | Nascent | High-value, low-mass specialty products |

> **Bottom line:** The 2026–2036 stack is **layered and multi-clock**. Bet hard on the intelligence layer, physical AI, digital twins and additive (scaling now); pilot quantum and in-space deliberately (emerging/nascent). IoT/edge data is the common fuel — own it.

## 5. Building next-generation manufacturing: architecture, data, roadmap

AI-native manufacturing is **80% data-and-organization, 20% algorithms.** A reference architecture:

1. **Connectivity & edge layer** — instrument assets (IoT/sensors), capture high-frequency data at the edge, compute where latency matters.
2. **Unified data foundation** — break OT/IT silos. A **unified namespace** + **industrial DataOps** create a single, contextualized, real-time data fabric so that AI, twins and agents draw from one trusted source (Deloitte; HiveMQ; Databricks industrial reference architecture).
3. **Digital-twin & simulation layer** — model assets, lines and networks; rehearse decisions before execution.
4. **AI & agent layer** — analytical AI (still the workhorse: ~77% of leading-site top-five use cases vs ~9% generative), generative copilots, and agentic orchestration.
5. **Human-in-the-loop & governance layer** — exception handling, safety, model monitoring, cyber, and clear accountability for autonomous decisions.

**Maturity path (Microsoft / IoT Analytics framing):** *Connected → Visible → Predictive → Autonomous.* Most manufacturers sit at **Connected/Visible**; the strategic goal of the decade is to climb to **Predictive** broadly and **Autonomous** in targeted high-value processes. The recurring failure mode is **"pilot purgatory"** — disconnected proofs-of-concept that never scale because the **data foundation and operating model weren't built first**.

**Roadmap (pragmatic sequencing):**
1. **Foundations first:** data architecture, connectivity, cyber, governance.
2. **Scale analytics + copilots:** capture near-term productivity/quality wins (these fund the journey).
3. **Twin the critical processes:** decisioning, not just dashboards.
4. **Introduce agents under human oversight:** start with low-risk, high-frequency workflows.
5. **Layer in physical AI** where tasks are variable/unstructured.
6. **Govern continuously:** treat models, agents and data as living assets.

> **Bottom line:** You cannot buy your way to an AI-native plant. **Fix the data foundation and operating model first**, scale the wins that pay for the journey, and introduce autonomy where the risk/reward is clearest.

## 6. What work will look like: human–machine collaboration

The evidence is consistent and, for many, reassuring: **AI augments more than it replaces** in manufacturing.

- **More than 81% of manufacturing task-hours are expected to remain human-driven** (Deloitte). Uniquely human capabilities — **creativity, collaboration, critical thinking, adaptability, emotional intelligence** — stay essential.
- WEF's skills analysis is even sharper: of **2,800+ skills assessed, zero** were rated "very high capacity" for substitution by current GenAI, and **69%** had "very low" or "low" substitution capacity — GenAI is limited where work needs **physical execution, nuanced judgment or hands-on application.**
- The work itself **moves up the value chain.** As autonomous systems absorb repetitive tasks, humans concentrate on **supervision, exception handling, optimization, maintenance of intelligent systems, and continuous improvement.** Deloitte's gen-AI product work describes the new unit of work as a **triad — engineers, operators and AI systems jointly shaping strategy in real time.**

**New and growing roles** (illustrative): AI/automation operations specialists, robotics & cobot technicians, digital-twin and simulation engineers, industrial data engineers/scientists, MLOps for the shop floor, AI-governance and safety leads, cybersecurity-OT specialists, and "hybrid" frontline operators fluent in reading AI-generated insight. **Declining emphasis:** purely manual, repetitive tasks (WEF flags the *first-ever net decline* in the importance of manual dexterity/endurance/precision).

> **Bottom line:** The 2036 shop floor is **smaller, more skilled and more collaborative** — fewer hands on repetitive tasks, more people supervising, maintaining and improving intelligent systems.

## 7. Skills for the future workforce

The headline tension: demand is rising for skilled workers exactly as the supply gap widens.

- **The gap:** Deloitte/The Manufacturing Institute project a **net need of ~3.8M manufacturing workers (US, 2024–2033)**, of which **~1.9M could go unfilled** without action — an evolution of the earlier "**2.1M unfilled by 2030, ~$1T cost**" estimate. Each manufacturing worker supports ~4.8 jobs elsewhere, so the shortfall ripples economy-wide.
- **Skill churn:** WEF (*Future of Jobs 2025*) — employers expect **39% of core skills to change by 2030** (down from 44% in 2023 as continuous-learning programs mature; **50%** of workers now train as part of long-term strategies, up from 41%).
- **Fastest-growing skills:** **AI & big data** (the single fastest-growing), networks & cybersecurity, technological literacy — *plus* **creative thinking, resilience/flexibility/agility, curiosity & lifelong learning, leadership & social influence.** In Advanced Manufacturing specifically, employers still prize **manual dexterity/precision** (~25% call it core) — but increasingly **combined with** technological literacy and problem-solving.
- **The pragmatic reality on the floor:** NAM's 2026 survey finds manufacturers prioritize **practical AI capability** — working alongside AI-enabled equipment and interpreting AI-generated insight — over deep AI expertise. Yet **~40%+ provide no AI training to frontline staff yet**, and **22.6%** say it's "too early" to define the skills needed. **Data literacy is now essential from line operator to plant manager.**

**The skills agenda for 2026–2036:**
- **Technical:** data literacy, AI/automation fluency, robotics, digital-twin/simulation, OT cybersecurity, additive/advanced-materials engineering.
- **Human/durable:** critical thinking, creativity, adaptability, collaboration, communication, systems thinking.
- **Hybrid:** the highest-value workers blend **hands-on manufacturing skill + digital fluency + problem-solving.**

> **Bottom line:** The binding constraint of the decade is **people, not machines.** Winners will treat **continuous reskilling (microlearning, employer-led partnerships, earn-while-you-learn)** as core infrastructure — as fundamental as the data platform.

## 8. Compensation, incentives and organizational structure

*(This is the most thinly-evidenced area in the source base; treat as directional/qualitative, triangulated from labor-market data and org-design research rather than hard pay benchmarks.)*

**Organizational structure** is being **rewired around digital workflows, not departmental silos.** NAM (Jan 2026): *"Manufacturers are reorganizing teams around digital workflows rather than traditional department boundaries. Cross-functional groups with engineering, operations, IT and data roles are becoming standard."* Practical implications:

- **Cross-functional pods** (engineering + operations + IT + data) own processes end-to-end and persist for ongoing optimization, not just project delivery.
- **OT and IT converge** — historically separate organizations merge or tightly integrate; a senior data/AI leadership function gains real shop-floor authority.
- **Flatter, faster** structures designed to "fail fast, scale easily, adapt without stalling," with **adaptive leadership** and **data literacy** expected across roles.

**Compensation & incentives** (directional):
- **Wage premium for hybrid skills.** As routine roles shrink and AI/robotics/data roles grow, pay tilts toward **scarce technical + digital + problem-solving** profiles. Even entry-level manufacturing wages already run well above local minimums (e.g., median team-assembler wage ~$15.55 vs $7.25 federal floor); the skilled premium widens further.
- **Incentives realign to new outcomes.** As business models become outcome- and uptime-based (§3), expect incentives to follow — rewarding **availability, quality yield, safety, continuous-improvement contribution and successful human-AI collaboration**, not just volume/throughput.
- **Retention economics dominate.** With ~1.9M roles at risk of going unfilled, **retention, upskilling pathways and culture** become compensation strategy — total rewards increasingly bundle **learning, flexibility and career mobility** alongside pay.

> **Bottom line:** Org charts reorganize around **data and workflows**; pay and incentives tilt toward **hybrid skills and new outcomes (uptime, quality, collaboration).** *(Hard, global pay-structure benchmarks remain a genuine evidence gap — see §12.)*

## 9. From legacy automation to AI-native: the transition

Traditional automation is **deterministic and rigid** — fixed PLCs, hard-coded lines, islands of control optimized once. AI-native operations are **adaptive and software-defined.** The transition is as much organizational as technical.

**Where most sit today:** strong investment *intent* but limited scaling. **80%** of executives plan ≥20% of improvement budgets on smart manufacturing (Deloitte) — but spend skews to **foundational tools** (automation hardware, analytics, sensors, cloud), and **63% prioritize automation mid-term while only 38% target a fully hyper-automated factory** (Accenture). Most are early on the maturity curve; the gap to the 238 Lighthouses is wide.

**A transition playbook:**
1. **Start with the data foundation, not the algorithm.** Unified namespace + DataOps to unsilo OT/IT (the #1 reason pilots fail to scale).
2. **Make automation software-defined.** Move from hard-coded lines toward reconfigurable, digitally-orchestrated cells; wrap legacy assets with sensors and connectivity rather than ripping-and-replacing.
3. **Scale the wins that pay for the journey.** Analytical AI and copilots first (quality, predictive maintenance, throughput) — these fund the harder steps.
4. **Twin before you automate.** Simulate changes; de-risk capital.
5. **Add agents and physical AI under human oversight**, expanding autonomy as trust and guardrails mature.
6. **Re-skill and re-org in parallel** — **70% of managers call workforce transformation the single most critical success factor** for the 2040 vision. Technology without the operating-model and people change stalls in pilot purgatory.

**Watch-outs:** complacency once shocks fade; over-indexing on tech buy vs. data/people; under-investing in OT cybersecurity as connectivity (and attack surface) expands.

> **Bottom line:** The migration is **evolutionary, not rip-and-replace** — wrap and augment legacy assets, fix data and organization first, and let early AI wins fund the climb to autonomy.

## 10. Products and costing in the AI era

### 10.1 How products change

- **Design compresses dramatically.** Generative/agentic AI shrinks R&D from **years to months**; in pharma, average prototype cycles cut **up to 70%**; in medtech, AI could cut **R&D cost up to 20%** (~**$300M** for a large player over 2–3 years) (Deloitte, Feb 2026). Three core capabilities drive it: **simulation, prediction, optimization.**
- **Products become software-defined, sensor-rich and updatable** — value (and margin) migrates to software, data and post-sale outcomes (enabling §3's business models).
- **Mass personalization becomes default** (NIO's 3.6M+ configurations) rather than a premium exception.
- **The design workflow becomes a human-AI triad** — engineers + operators + AI jointly shaping not just the product but the manufacturing strategy in real time. *Caveat:* physical-product AI lags digital because real products live in complex multi-physics environments and must integrate with CAD/CAE/PLM — only **~6% of manufacturers** use gen AI in product engineering today, though **~a quarter expect to within two years.**

### 10.2 How costing and pricing change

- **From unit cost to cost-to-serve.** Personalization and servitization make a single per-unit cost meaningless; firms must model **the cost to serve each customer/configuration over a lifecycle.** AI makes this granular, real-time costing feasible for the first time.
- **From cost-plus to value/outcome-based pricing.** When you sell outcomes (uptime, units produced, business results), price is anchored to **value delivered**, not cost incurred — requiring AI-driven understanding of usage, condition and realized value. (Simon-Kucher and others document this pricing-model shift in the AI age.)
- **Cost structure itself shifts.** Lower marginal labor and faster design lower some costs; but **data, AI, cybersecurity, software and continuous-learning** become significant new fixed/operating costs. **Total cost of ownership** thinking — including model maintenance, governance and reskilling — replaces simple capex-vs-opex framing.

> **Bottom line:** Products become **personalized, software-defined and continuously updated**; costing moves from **per-unit** to **per-customer, per-outcome, per-lifecycle** — a finance and pricing transformation as deep as the operational one.

## 11. Three scenarios for 2036

| | **Accelerated** | **Base case (most likely)** | **Stalled** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Autonomy** | "Self-managing" common in leaders; software-defined automation mainstream | Leaders reach "self-directing"; broad market at "predictive/co-adaptive" | Most stuck at "assisted"; pilots don't scale |
| **Workforce** | Aggressive reskilling closes much of the gap | Persistent skills shortage; partial mitigation | ~1.9M+ roles unfilled; growth capped |
| **Supply chain** | Resilient regional hubs + agentic control towers standard | China+1 + selective regionalization; agentic SCM scaling | Complacency returns; fragility persists |
| **Gap** | Narrows as tools democratize | **Leader–laggard gap widens** | Gap widens sharply; laggards exit |

The research points most strongly to the **base case**: real, accelerating transformation **concentrated among leaders**, with the **leader–laggard gap as the decade's defining dynamic.**

## 12. Risks, caveats and open questions

**Caveats (carry these with every number above):**
- **Selection / self-report bias.** Headline metrics (+40% productivity, –48% lead time; NIO/Foxconn/Amazon) are **panel-selected, largely self-reported showcase results** from pioneers — leading-edge averages, *not* industry norms.
- **Intent ≠ deployment.** Many figures (Deloitte 80%; physical-AI 9%→22%; Gartner 60% by 2030) measure **stated intent/sentiment** from proprietary, non-replicable surveys; several are **US-scoped**.
- **Single-vendor framing.** The IFM/agentic stack framing leans on **Siemens** foresight (properly attributed).
- **Time-sensitivity.** Network size (220→238) and use-case splits move fast and may already be stale; McKinsey's 5–15-yr window is an explicitly conditional scenario.

**Genuine evidence gaps** (where this report is thinnest and where I'd recommend deeper primary research before betting):
1. **Hard, global compensation & org-structure benchmarks** for AI-augmented manufacturing roles — qualitative direction is clear; quantified pay structures are not well-evidenced.
2. **Realistic, quantified 2026–2036 impact of quantum and in-space manufacturing** beyond market-sizing forecasts.
3. **Concrete cost-to-serve / outcome-pricing transition mechanics** at scale — the economics are described directionally more than measured.
