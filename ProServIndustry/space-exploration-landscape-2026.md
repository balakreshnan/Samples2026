# The Space Exploration Landscape: Companies, Technology & Business Value (2026)

*A detailed analysis of who is doing what across the global space industry, the technology each relies on, the business value they create, and how the vendor categories differ.*

**Prepared:** June 29, 2026
**Scope:** Commercial operators and national agencies active in launch, satellite services, Earth observation, human spaceflight, lunar/planetary exploration, in-space manufacturing, orbital servicing, and space tourism.
**Sourcing note:** Figures are drawn from public company disclosures, agency announcements, and industry trade reporting current to mid-2026. Privately held companies do not publish audited results, so their revenue/funding numbers are management disclosures or media estimates and should be treated as approximate. Launch and mission dates are planning targets and slip frequently.

---

## 1. Executive Summary

The space sector in 2026 is no longer a single "rocket" business. It has split into roughly **eight distinct industries**, each with different technology, customers, capital needs, and economics:

1. **Launch providers** — getting mass to orbit
2. **Satellite communications / mega-constellations** — broadband and connectivity from space
3. **Earth observation & geospatial intelligence** — selling imagery and, increasingly, AI-derived answers
4. **Human spaceflight & commercial space stations** — the post-ISS race
5. **Lunar & planetary exploration** — Moon landers, sample return, deep-space science
6. **In-space manufacturing** — making products in microgravity
7. **Orbital logistics, servicing & debris removal** — "roadside services" in orbit
8. **Space tourism** — suborbital and orbital experiences

**Cross-cutting themes:**

- **Reusability turned launch into a throughput business.** SpaceX flew roughly 129 missions in 2025 — about one every two days — and captured over half of global launches. Rivals now compete against an industrial system, not a single rocket.
- **The value is migrating from hardware to data and services.** In Earth observation, "customers don't want pixels, they want answers" — AI analytics is now the main revenue battleground. The same logic (recurring revenue beats one-off sales) is reshaping every category.
- **Government remains the anchor customer** in most categories — national security, NASA/ESA science, and defense contracts underwrite the commercial buildout. Some categories (debris removal) have essentially *no* private business model yet.
- **2026 is being called "the Year of the Moon"** — Artemis II flew the first crewed lunar flyby since 1972, and a wave of commercial and national landers is targeting the lunar south pole and its suspected water ice.

## 3. Detailed Analysis by Category

### Category 1 — Launch Providers

The foundational layer. Whoever controls cheap, frequent access to orbit shapes every category above it.

#### SpaceX (private — Hawthorne, CA)
- **What they do:** The dominant global launch provider plus the world's largest satellite operator (Starlink). In 2025 the company flew roughly 129 missions (about 125 Falcon 9 and 4 Starship), averaging one launch every ~2.2 days, and accounted for over half of all global launches in the first half of the year.
- **Tech stack:** *Falcon 9 Block 5* — partially reusable, kerosene/LOX (Merlin engines); cited ~99.79% mission success and 384+ booster reflights with a 100% reflight success rate as of early 2025. *Falcon Heavy* for heavy GTO/exotic orbits. *Starship/Super Heavy* — fully reusable, methane/LOX (Raptor 3, full-flow staged combustion), ~150 t to LEO; "Mechazilla" catch tower; Flight 12 (Starship V3, ~407 ft) flew in May 2026. Selected as NASA's Artemis Human Landing System.
- **Business value:** Reusability collapsed cost-per-kg toward ~$1,500–2,700 (versus tens of thousands historically), letting SpaceX undercut all rivals. Crucially, **Starlink is an internal anchor customer** — it keeps factories and pads in constant motion and funds Starship/Mars development. The model is an integrated "launch machine," not a single vehicle.

#### Blue Origin (private — Kent, WA; Jeff Bezos)
- **What they do:** Orbital and suborbital launch, lunar landers, an orbital tug, and a planned satcom network.
- **Tech stack:** *New Glenn* — reusable first stage, seven BE-4 engines (methane/LOX), 7-meter fairing; NG-2 (Nov 2025) deployed NASA's ESCAPADE Mars probes and landed its booster; NG-3 flew Apr 2026; an NG-4 hotfire anomaly in late May 2026 set the program back. *New Shepard* — suborbital tourism/research (38 flights), **paused for ≥2 years in Jan 2026** to redirect resources to lunar. *Blue Moon MK1 "Endurance"* — uncrewed cargo lander (~3,000 kg, BE-7 LOX/LH2 engine), selected as the lander for NASA's first Moon Base mission. *Blue Moon MK2* — crewed lander for Artemis. *Blue Ring* — orbital transfer/space-domain-awareness platform. *TeraWave* — announced 6 Tbps satcom network.
- **Business value:** Positioning as a vertically integrated heavy-lift + cislunar infrastructure provider. As the only current U.S. heavy-lift vehicle besides Falcon/Starship sized for the largest lunar payloads, New Glenn's return to flight is strategically vital to both Blue Origin and NASA.

#### Rocket Lab (NASDAQ: RKLB — Long Beach, CA; Sir Peter Beck)
- **What they do:** The world's second-most-active launch provider and a fast-growing end-to-end space-systems company.
- **Tech stack:** *Electron* — the most-flown small orbital rocket (87+ launches; 21 in 2025 with a 100% success rate; ~300 kg to LEO). *Neutron* — reusable medium-lift (13 t to LEO, methane/LOX, nine Archimedes engines, novel "Hungry Hippo" fairing), debut targeted mid-2026 from Wallops, Virginia. *HASTE* — hypersonic test variant for defense. *Space Systems* division builds spacecraft, solar, reaction wheels, and components.
- **Business value:** Record Q1 2026 revenue of ~$200.3M (+63.5% YoY), $2.2B backlog, 70+ mission manifest, ~$37B market cap. Won an $816M Space Development Agency contract (18 missile-warning satellites) and a role in the "Golden Dome" Space-Based Interceptor program. Strategy is **vertical integration** (acquisitions of Mynaric, Geost, Motiv) to serve launch + satellites + components for commercial, civil, and national-security markets.

#### United Launch Alliance (Boeing–Lockheed JV — Centennial, CO)
- **What they do:** Premium, high-reliability launch for U.S. national security and high-energy/exotic orbits.
- **Tech stack:** *Vulcan Centaur* — expendable heavy-lift, twin BE-4 engines + up to four GEM-63XL solid boosters, high-energy Centaur V upper stage (~11,700 kg to GTO direct-to-GEO capability). Legacy *Atlas V* still flying (Amazon Kuiper, Starliner crew).
- **Business value:** The incumbent for the most demanding National Security Space Launch missions where precision and assured access matter more than price. Competing for NSSL Phase 2/3 awards against SpaceX.

#### Arianespace / ArianeGroup (Évry, France)
- **What they do:** Europe's sovereign access to space.
- **Tech stack:** *Ariane 6* — A62 (two boosters) and A64 (four boosters) variants, hydrogen/oxygen Vulcain 2.1 first stage + restartable Vinci upper stage; ~$88M for an A62; first flew July 2024. Now flying EUMETSAT weather satellites, Galileo, and Amazon Leo.
- **Business value:** Guarantees European institutional and sovereign launch independence rather than competing purely on cost.

#### Emerging launchers
- **Relativity Space** — *Terran R*, large reusable rocket built around 3D-printed structures (in development).
- **Firefly Aerospace** — *Alpha* small-lift launcher plus the *Blue Ghost* lunar lander line (see Category 5).
- **Sierra Space** — *Dream Chaser* spaceplane (cargo to ISS first), launching on Vulcan.

### Category 2 — Satellite Communications & Mega-Constellations

Connectivity is the single largest commercial revenue engine in space today.

- **Starlink (SpaceX):** 5,000+ satellites in orbit, ~72% of the U.S. residential satellite-broadband market and 2.4M+ U.S. households; expanding into **direct-to-cell** (eliminating dead zones via partnerships with T-Mobile, Rogers, KDDI). Analysts project the D2C line alone could reach ~$10B/year by 2030. Starlink is also SpaceX's funding engine.
- **Amazon Leo (formerly Project Kuiper):** Full-scale deployment began April 2025; ~300+ satellites across 11 missions in its first year, making it the third-largest constellation, with 100+ launches secured (spread across Ariane 6, ULA Atlas V, and New Glenn). Strategy leverages Amazon's enterprise/AWS ecosystem and retail distribution.
- **Eutelsat OneWeb:** A combined GEO + LEO operator targeting enterprise, government, and backhaul, with a European sovereignty angle.
- **AST SpaceMobile:** Building large *BlueBird* satellites for **direct smartphone connectivity** (the Block 2 BlueBird is a New Glenn payload).
- **Blue Origin TeraWave:** Announced a 6 Tbps space-based network for symmetrical high-speed connectivity.
- **China's Thousand Sails / SatNet (Guowang):** State-backed constellations planned at ~13,000+ satellites — a strategic competitor to Starlink.
- **Business value:** Recurring subscription revenue at consumer and enterprise scale; rural/maritime/aviation broadband; resilient/sovereign communications. The risk is **capital intensity and orbital congestion**.

### Category 3 — Earth Observation & Geospatial Intelligence

A rare space subsector generating **recurring commercial revenue at scale** — estimated at ~$17B in 2026 for the full ecosystem (or ~$7.7B for data/services narrowly), growing ~8–12% annually. The decisive shift: selling *decisions*, not pixels. The imagery-analytics segment is now ~41% of the market.

| Company | Modality | Constellation | Niche / customers |
|---------|----------|---------------|-------------------|
| **Planet Labs** (NYSE: PL) | Optical (+ hyperspectral) | ~200 Dove/SuperDove (3 m daily), SkySat (50 cm), Pelican (30 cm next-gen), Tanager (hyperspectral) | Daily global revisit; agriculture, defense, finance, climate. ~$280M annualized revenue; "Planet Insights" analytics platform |
| **Maxar Intelligence** (Vivace-owned) | Optical (very high res) | WorldView Legion + WorldView-3 (~30 cm) | Highest-resolution commercial imagery; defense & intelligence default |
| **BlackSky** (NYSE: BKSY) | Optical (rapid revisit) | ~20 satellites | "Image this exact spot every 90 minutes"; tasking-first; DoD/intel. ~$107M 2025 revenue; Spectra analytics |
| **ICEYE** (Finland) | SAR (radar) | ~38 satellites (segment volume leader) | All-weather/night imaging; flood mapping, insurance, defense (Bundeswehr €1.76B) |
| **Capella Space** (acquired by IonQ, 2025) | SAR | ~10 satellites | U.S.-flag SAR for NRO/DoD/NGA; rapid tasking |
| **Umbra** | SAR | small fleet | Resolution leader (~16 cm sub-pixel) |
| **Spire Global** | RF / weather | ~100 satellites | AIS (ships), ADS-B (aircraft), GNSS-RO (weather) |
| **Satellogic / Synspective / Pixxel** | Optical / SAR / hyperspectral | growing | Lower-cost imagery; hyperspectral (minerals, methane) |

- **Tech stack:** Six modalities — optical, SAR, RF, hyperspectral, thermal, and atmospheric — increasingly paired with **on-satellite AI processing** (to cut downlink volume) and ground-based foundation models (e.g., IBM–NASA Prithvi, Clay). Sub-3 m optical is standard; SAR is the fastest-growing segment because it sees through clouds and at night.
- **Business value:** Change detection, vessel/vehicle counting, deforestation and methane alerts, disaster and insurance assessment, supply-chain and financial intelligence, defense ISR. The structural advantage is **recurring access + analytics subscriptions**; the bottleneck is no longer building satellites but selling data into analytics workflows.

### Category 4 — Human Spaceflight & Commercial Space Stations

The ISS is scheduled to deorbit around 2030–2031 (via a dedicated SpaceX-built deorbit vehicle). NASA's Commercial LEO Destinations program — reshaped in March 2026 into an "Ignition" framework — is funding successors (Phase 1 ~$415M+; Phase 2 ~$1–1.5B, awards expected in 2026).

| Contender | Architecture | Status / first station | Funding | Differentiator |
|-----------|--------------|------------------------|---------|----------------|
| **Vast** (Long Beach) | Independent single-launch modules | Haven-1 (~18 m³, 4 crew) targeting Q1 2027 on Falcon 9; Haven-2 multi-module from 2028 | $1B+ raised ($500M round Mar 2026) | Only pure-play with flight heritage (Haven Demo, 2024–25); fastest path; long-term artificial-gravity vision |
| **Axiom Space** (Houston) | Modules attach to ISS, then detach as free-flyer | "Hab One" attaches ~2026 | ~$505M+ (est. $3B valuation) | **Only one already flying** (Ax-1…Ax-4 private missions, ~$55M/seat); makes the AxEMU Artemis spacesuit; real revenue today |
| **Orbital Reef** (Blue Origin + Sierra Space) | Multi-tenant "business park" w/ inflatable LIFE habitat | ~2028+ | Self-funded + CLD ($130M design) | Broadest partner coalition; Dream Chaser crew access; but lagging schedule, depends on New Glenn |
| **Starlab** (Voyager + Airbus) | Single large rigid module on Starship | ~2029 | CLD ($160M) | Strongest international/ESA partnership; Airbus ISS-module heritage |
| **Boeing Starliner** | Crew capsule (not a station) | Crewed certification ongoing post-anomalies | — | NASA's second commercial crew vehicle alongside Crew Dragon |
| **China Tiangong** (CMSA) | Government station | **Operational since 2021**, expanding to 6 modules | State | The only non-ISS station flying; opening to international payloads |

- **Tech stack:** Pressurized modules, life support / ECLSS, EVA suits (Axiom AxEMU), docking systems, and crew transport on SpaceX Crew Dragon or (future) Dream Chaser.
- **Business value:** Microgravity research, pharmaceutical and materials R&D, sovereign/government astronaut programs, media, and tourism. Axiom monetizes today via private missions; the others are largely pre-revenue and dependent on NASA anchor funding.

### Category 5 — Lunar & Planetary Exploration

The densest area of activity in 2026–2027, split between **commercial landers** (mostly under NASA's CLPS program) and **national flagship missions**.

**Commercial lunar landers (CLPS):**
- **Intuitive Machines** — IM-1 (Feb 2024) was the first U.S. soft landing since Apollo (it tipped on landing); IM-2 and IM-3 (Reiner Gamma, 2026) followed. Won a $180.4M NASA award for a larger cargo-class lander delivering seven payloads to the south pole.
- **Astrobotic** — *Griffin-1* lander targeting the south pole region (NET fall 2026); earlier *Peregrine*.
- **Firefly Aerospace** — *Blue Ghost*; Mission 2 targets the lunar far side in 2026.
- **ispace** (Japan) — commercial lunar landers (after earlier landing attempts).
- **Blue Origin Blue Moon MK1 "Endurance"** — see Category 1; NASA's first Moon Base lander, potentially launching by end of 2026.
- **Business model:** NASA buys delivery *as a service* rather than building every lander — lowering cost and seeding a cislunar economy. Commercial demand beyond government is still nascent.

**National agency flagship programs:**
- **NASA — Artemis:** *Artemis II* flew the first crewed lunar flyby since 1972 (splashdown ~April 10, 2026; a ~252,756-mile human distance record). Uses the SLS rocket + Orion capsule. *Artemis III* has been reworked into a LEO lander-demonstration mission, with the crewed landing relying on SpaceX's Starship HLS and slipping toward 2027+. Persistent vulnerability: SLS's ~$4–5B/year cost.
- **China — Chang'e:** Arguably the most consistently on-schedule lunar program. Chang'e 6 (2024) achieved the first-ever **far-side sample return**; *Chang'e 7* (2026) targets the south pole with an orbiter, lander, rover, and a "hopper" to probe permanently shadowed craters for water ice (carrying international payloads).
- **India — ISRO:** *Gaganyaan G1* uncrewed human-spaceflight test (~March 2026, carrying the Vyommitra humanoid robot) on the human-rated LVM3; *Chandrayaan-4* lunar sample return (~2027–28); NewSpace India Limited commercializing LVM3.
- **JAXA (Japan):** *MMX* — Martian Moons eXploration, a Phobos sample-return mission.
- **ESA (Europe):** *Hera* (asteroid-deflection follow-up at Didymos/Dimorphos), *BepiColombo* (Mercury orbit insertion, with JAXA), *PLATO* (exoplanet telescope).
- **NASA science:** *Nancy Grace Roman Space Telescope* (infrared/exoplanets), *ESCAPADE* (Mars twins, launched on New Glenn).
- **Business value:** Pure science, national prestige, resource prospecting (lunar water ice = future propellant), and planetary defense — funded by taxpayers, not commercial revenue, but anchoring demand for the entire commercial supply chain.

### Category 6 — In-Space Manufacturing (Microgravity Economy)

Producing materials that are impossible or inferior to make on Earth. Projected to exceed $10B by ~2035 (≈35% CAGR), though still early.

- **Varda Space Industries** — Autonomous free-flying capsules that manufacture **pharmaceuticals** in microgravity and return them via reentry capsule; completed the first commercial pharmaceutical manufacturing mission in 2024. Reentry/hypersonic data is a dual-use byproduct sold to defense.
- **Redwire** — Produces **ZBLAN optical fiber** (superior to terrestrial fiber) and bioprinting research aboard the ISS; broad space-infrastructure portfolio.
- **Space Forge** (UK) — Developing **reusable return capsules** for semiconductor/advanced-materials production.
- **Tech stack:** Small automated orbital platforms or station-based payload racks, plus reentry/recovery capsules.
- **Business value:** High-value-per-gram products (specialty pharma crystals, ultra-pure fiber, novel alloys/semiconductors). The thesis depends on falling launch costs and validated quality advantages; commercial demand is still being proven.

### Category 7 — Orbital Logistics, Servicing & Debris Removal

"Roadside services in space" — tugs, life-extension, inspection, and active debris removal (ADR). Backdrop: 34,000+ tracked objects >10 cm and an estimated 128M+ untracked fragments, with Kessler-syndrome cascade risk.

- **Astroscale** (Tokyo) — The best-funded ADR/servicing company (~$396.8M). *ADRAS-J* (launched Feb 2024) performed the world's first commercial close-proximity inspection of a large piece of debris — approaching within 15 m of a ~3-ton H-IIA rocket stage — and began controlled deorbit in March 2026 after 293 days. *ADRAS-J2* (FY2027–28) will attempt actual capture and deorbit. *ELSA-M* (2026) targets cooperative multi-satellite removal; *COSMIC* (UK) and a patented distributed "servicer + reentry shepherd" architecture extend the line.
- **ClearSpace** (Switzerland/Luxembourg) — Europe's flagship ADR effort. *ClearSpace-1* (ESA contract ~€86–100M) uses a four-armed robotic capture system, now targeting ESA's defunct PROBA-1 after the original target was struck by debris; also the UK's *CLEAR* mission. Also pursuing in-orbit inspection and life-extension.
- **D-Orbit** (Italy) — *ION Satellite Carrier* space tug for precise last-mile satellite deployment and propulsive decommissioning.
- **Impulse Space** (US) — Orbital transfer vehicles for in-space mobility / last-mile delivery to precise orbits.
- **Tech stack:** Rendezvous & proximity operations (RPO), autonomous navigation (cameras/LiDAR), robotic arms / magnetic docking plates, electric and chemical propulsion.
- **Business value & the catch:** Technically the barriers are being overcome, but **there is essentially no commercial business model yet** — a removal costs ~$100–500M while the diffuse annual benefit (~$86–103M across *all* operators) is shared too thinly for any one operator to pay. The "market" is almost entirely government-funded (JAXA, ESA, DARPA, U.S. Space Force). **Regulation is the demand driver:** the FCC's 5-year deorbit rule and ESA's Zero Debris Charter (no new debris by 2030). Satellite life-extension and on-orbit refueling/servicing are the nearer-term *commercial* opportunities.

Maturing from billionaire joyrides toward a multi-provider market spanning ~$125K to $55M.

Suborbital economics depend on flight rate (the reason Virgin Galactic retired Unity for the higher-cadence Delta-class).

## 4. Comparison Across Vendor Categories

| Category | Capital intensity | Primary revenue model | Dominant customer | Commercial maturity | Key moat |
|----------|-------------------|-----------------------|-------------------|---------------------|----------|
| **Launch** | Very high | Per-launch fees + internal demand | Commercial + government + own constellation | High (SpaceX) → Emerging (others) | Reusability, cadence, operational learning curve |
| **Satellite comms** | Very high | Recurring subscriptions | Consumers, enterprise, government | High (Starlink) → Scaling (Kuiper) | Spectrum, scale, launch access |
| **Earth observation** | Medium | Data + analytics subscriptions | Defense/intel, then commercial verticals | High (recurring revenue) | Constellation + analytics/AI software |
| **Human spaceflight / stations** | Very high | Seats, research time, NASA service contracts | NASA + sovereign astronaut programs | Low–Medium (Axiom flying; others pre-revenue) | Flight heritage, NASA anchor funding |
| **Lunar / planetary** | High | Government service contracts (CLPS) + agency budgets | NASA, ESA, CNSA, ISRO | Low (government-funded) | Landing capability, agency relationships |
| **In-space manufacturing** | Medium | Product sales (pharma, fiber, materials) | Pharma, materials, defense | Very low (early demos) | Reentry/recovery + proven product advantage |
| **Orbital servicing / debris** | Medium–High | Government contracts (no real commercial model yet) | JAXA, ESA, DARPA, Space Force | Very low | RPO/robotics IP; regulation-driven demand |
| **Space tourism** | Medium–High | Per-seat ticket sales | Wealthy individuals; researchers | Low–Medium | Reusability, flight cadence, brand/safety |

### How the categories differ — the key distinctions

1. **Who pays.** Comms and Earth observation have genuine *commercial* customers paying recurring fees. Lunar/planetary, debris removal, and (mostly) stations are still underwritten by **governments** — they are service contracts, not open markets.
2. **Hardware vs. data economics.** Launch, stations, and landers are capital-heavy *hardware* businesses with lumpy, project-based revenue. Earth observation and comms are increasingly **software/subscription** businesses where the recurring data relationship — not the satellite — is the asset.
3. **Maturity gradient.** Launch (SpaceX) and satellite broadband (Starlink) are profitable, high-volume industries. In-space manufacturing and debris removal are at the *demonstration* stage with unproven economics. Stations and lunar landers sit in between — real hardware, but dependent on NASA's transition funding.
4. **Moats.** In launch, the moat is a **compounding operational learning curve** (each flight improves the next) plus an internal anchor customer — which is why no rival has matched SpaceX's cadence. In Earth observation, the moat is shifting from the constellation to the **analytics layer**. In servicing, the "moat" (RPO robotics) exists but has no market to defend yet.
5. **Vertical integration vs. specialization.** Two opposing strategies are winning. **Vertical integrators** (SpaceX with Starlink; Rocket Lab buying its way across launch + satellites + components; Blue Origin spanning launch + landers + tugs + satcom) capture more of the stack and create internal demand. **Specialists** (ICEYE in SAR, Varda in pharma, Astroscale in servicing) win by being the deepest in one niche. The squeezed middle is generalist hardware vendors without a recurring-revenue layer.
6. **Government as catalyst vs. crutch.** National-security and NASA money *de-risks* commercial buildout (Rocket Lab's $816M SDA deal, Maxar's intel base, CLPS for landers). But categories that depend *entirely* on government (debris removal) lack a self-sustaining market — a strategic vulnerability if budgets tighten.

## 5. Cross-Cutting Market Context

- **Launch is the enabler.** Over 250 orbital launches were projected for 2026 — the busiest year ever. SpaceX alone accounts for the majority of payload mass delivered worldwide; China runs a 50+ launch national campaign.
- **The "answers, not pixels" shift** in Earth observation is the template the whole industry is following: move up the value stack from raw capability to AI-derived, decision-ready services with recurring revenue.
- **2026 = "Year of the Moon."** Artemis II's crewed flyby, Chang'e 7's south-pole campaign, and a cluster of commercial landers converge on the lunar south pole — driven by the prospect of water ice as in-situ propellant.
- **Sustainability is becoming regulated, not optional.** The FCC 5-year deorbit rule and ESA's Zero Debris Charter (2030) are creating the first real demand signals for servicing/deorbit.

---

## 6. Risks & Caveats

- **Schedule slip is the norm.** Nearly every date here is a planning target; deep-space and new-vehicle programs routinely slip (Neutron, New Glenn after its NG-4 anomaly, Artemis III, commercial stations).
- **Market concentration.** SpaceX's ~50%+ share of global launch and Starlink's broadband lead create single-point dependency for much of the industry (most station and lander programs rely on SpaceX or Blue Origin to reach orbit).
- **Unproven economics.** In-space manufacturing and debris removal have demonstrated *technology* but not a *commercial market*. Treat their large TAM projections with caution.
- **Private financials are estimates.** Funding/revenue/valuation figures for private firms (Vast, Axiom, ICEYE, Varda, Astroscale) are management disclosures or media reporting, not audited results.
- **Geopolitics.** Export controls, sovereign-capability demands (Europe, India), and counterspace activity (Russia, China) increasingly shape which vendors win which contracts.
